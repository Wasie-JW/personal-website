The government's bind

My section focused on the Brazilian government's structurally conflicted position: coffee exports are a key source of national revenue, which gives the state an economic incentive to keep production growing — even while it's also the body responsible for enforcing the labour and environmental laws that growth tends to violate.

  • The Ministry of Labour's public "Dirty List" names employers using slave-like labour practices, but enforcement is inconsistent.
  • Satellite monitoring is used to detect illegal deforestation — funded by the same coffee export revenue the government has an incentive to protect.
  • The government also navigates international pressure, including EU deforestation rules and U.S. trade actions tied to forced labour.

Applying theory: why the imbalance persists

The team's actor map showed unequal power. My theory section explained the mechanism behind it, drawing on two frameworks:

Actor-Network Theory (Latour)

No actor's strategy works in isolation — corporations, farmers, government, and communities are linked in a network where each actor's move shapes the others' options. Power in this network comes from the density of an actor's connections, not from any single attribute: corporations are powerful largely because they sit at the centre of consumer demand, financial capital, and infrastructure simultaneously.

Racial capitalism & the Capitalocene

Brazil's coffee economy has structural roots in 19th-century plantation systems, and documented modern cases of forced labour suggest meaningful continuity rather than a clean break from that history. Reading this through the Capitalocene frame — where capitalism itself, not "humanity" in the abstract, is the driver of ecological harm — helped explain why voluntary fixes like CSR certification tend to function as reputational cover rather than structural change.

Where we landed

The report's conclusion, which I helped develop, argues that the harms documented aren't accidents of an otherwise-functional system, but predictable outcomes of a market built to prioritise profit over people and ecosystems. Real change would require shifting the underlying power dynamics, not just adding more certifications on top of them — something we framed around the idea of sustainable degrowth: producing less, simplifying supply chains, and moving away from a profit-maximisation default.
