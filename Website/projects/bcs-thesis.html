Thesis — Determinantal Point Processes for politically diverse newsfeeds

Written analysis ML & NLP Python

The problem

News recommendation systems optimize user engagement at the cost of ideological diversity, reinforcing filter bubbles that narrow readers’ exposure to political perspectives. My thesis investigates how Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) can balance the trade-off between personalization and echo chamber formation in newsfeed recommendations.

Contributions

Three contributions are presented. First, a text-based Political Leaning Classifier (PLC) is developed using a regularized DistilBERT architecture, assigning continuous ideological scores to articles based solely on their content rather than publisher metadata. Second, a customizable DPP kernel framework is introduced that linearly combines semantic and political similarity through a tunable hyperparameter β, alongside an ε-greedy exploration variant designed to counteract candidate set bias in two-stage pipelines. Third, five recommendation models are benchmarked on the MIND-small dataset using accuracy, semantic diversity, and political diversity metrics.

  • MIND-small dataset used
  • Comparison of accuracy-based system and a DPP-based system
  • Evaluated offline, in a difficult setting, across 6 metrics

Results

Empirical results show that standalone DPP models achieve 75% higher political balance and 160% higher semantic diversity than a relevance-focused NAML-lite baseline, with no meaningful loss in recommendation accuracy under offline evaluation conditions. Hybrid models confirm that late-stage DPP reranking over an engagement-optimized shortlist measurably constrains diversity output. The ε-greedy variant successfully recovers this lost diversity by sampling outside the pre-filtered candidate set, though at the expense of the accuracy gains introduced by the first stage.

Key finding

Provided empirical evidence that DPPs offer a principled, tunable mechanism for mitigating filter bubbles in political news recommendations.

Limitations

Offline evaluation setting, with missing article bodies and an inherent dataset bias, were main causes for accuracy metrics <1% for each system. Additionally, the computational power difficulties were a reason for using a smaller dataset. In future, testing this design online, in an A/B user test, would provide more actionable insight about the properties of my proposed system.
